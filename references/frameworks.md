# Career Frameworks & Mental Models

A toolkit of frameworks used by top career advisors, executives, and thinkers. Apply these deliberately — name them when you use them, explain why they're relevant, and don't force a framework where it doesn't fit.

---

## 1. Ikigai (Purpose Intersection)
*From Japanese philosophy; popularized in career contexts by Ken Mogi and others*

**What it is:** A Venn diagram of four circles:
- What you **love** (passion)
- What you're **good at** (strength)
- What the **world needs** (relevance)
- What you can be **paid for** (viability)

The intersection of all four is *ikigai* — your reason for being / ideal career zone.

**When to use:** When someone feels generally unfulfilled but can't articulate why, or when exploring career directions from scratch.

**Honest limitation:** The "world needs" and "paid for" circles are often underweighted. Something can be deeply meaningful but unmarketable. Help the user think about whether their intersection is actually viable, not just emotionally resonant.

**Questions to ask:**
- What activities make you lose track of time?
- What do people consistently come to you for help with?
- If money weren't a factor, what would you do for free?
- What problems genuinely frustrate you that you want to solve?

## 2. The Hedgehog Concept
*From Jim Collins, "Good to Great"*

**What it is:** Three overlapping circles:
- What you can be **best in the world** at (not just good — genuinely excellent)
- What drives your **economic engine** (what others will pay for)
- What you are **deeply passionate** about

**When to use:** When someone has multiple interests and needs to focus, or when they're optimizing for long-term excellence rather than short-term options.

**Key distinction from Ikigai:** Collins emphasizes ruthless focus. The hedgehog doesn't do many things well — it does one thing supremely well. This framework pushes toward specialization.

**Honest limitation:** Very few people in early-mid career can honestly answer "what can I be best in the world at?" — the question is better framed as "what is my most defensible area of unique advantage?"

---

## 3. Career Capital Theory
*From Cal Newport, "So Good They Can't Ignore You"*

**What it is:** Rare and valuable skills are the currency of great careers. You earn them through deliberate practice in adjacent, uncomfortable work — not by "following your passion."

**When to use:** When someone feels stuck in a mediocre role but isn't sure how to advance, or when they want to justify "staying" somewhere while building toward something bigger.

**Core insight:** Passion often follows mastery, not the other way around. Invest in becoming excellent at something valuable; the meaning and freedom follow.

**Questions to ask:**
- What rare and valuable skill could you realistically build in the next 2–3 years in your current role?
- Are you doing deliberate practice (working at the edge of your ability) or just logging time?
- What career capital would you need to acquire to have the options you want?

## 4. Regret Minimization Framework
*From Jeff Bezos*

**What it is:** Project yourself to age 80, looking back. Which decision would you regret more — trying and failing, or never trying? Choose the path that minimizes regret from that vantage point.

**When to use:** When someone is paralyzed by fear of a risky move (starting a company, switching fields, taking a pay cut for meaning).

**Honest limitation:** This framework has a bias toward action and risk-taking. It can underweight practical concerns (financial stability, family obligations). Balance it with BATNA.

---

## 5. BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)
*From Harvard Negotiation Project; Roger Fisher & William Ury*

**What it is:** Before making any career decision, know your Best Alternative. Your BATNA determines your leverage and your floor.

**When to use:** Job offer decisions, salary negotiations, deciding whether to leave a role.

**Application:**
- What is your actual BATNA right now? (Not your desired outcome — your real alternative if this deal falls through)
- How does this offer compare to your BATNA?
- How can you *improve* your BATNA before making the decision? (Other offers, skill-building, savings runway)

**Key insight:** A bad BATNA makes you desperate. The most important career move is often improving your BATNA before you need it.

---

## 6. Designing Your Life — Odyssey Plans
*From Bill Burnett & Dave Evans, Stanford d.school*

**What it is:** Write out three distinct 5-year plans:
1. **Current path extended** — What happens if you keep doing what you're doing?
2. **Plan B** — A compelling alternative if Plan A became impossible
3. **The wild card** — What you'd do if no one would judge you and success was guaranteed

Rate each on: Resources needed, Confidence level, Engagement/energy, Coherence with your values.

**When to use:** When someone is stuck in one way of imagining their future, or when they feel like they have "no options."

**Key insight:** Most people overestimate how trapped they are. Having three plans simultaneously reduces anxiety and opens up creative possibility.

## 7. The Skills Stack / Personal Monopoly
*From Scott Adams (Dilbert creator) and David Perell*

**What it is:** You don't need to be #1 in any single skill. Combine 2–3 skills where you're in the top 25% each — the intersection becomes rare and uniquely yours.

**Scott Adams' version:** "The most successful people I know are good at two or more things. The combination is rare enough to make them valuable."

**David Perell's "Personal Monopoly":** Build a unique worldview and body of work at the intersection of your skills and interests that no one else occupies.

**When to use:** When someone feels "not the best at anything" — this reframes their stack of experiences as an asset.

**Questions to ask:**
- What 2–3 skill areas do you have unusual combinations of?
- Is there a problem or audience that this combination uniquely serves?
- What content or work could only *you* produce?

## 8. Energy Audit
*From Tony Schwartz, "The Power of Full Engagement"; also used by executive coaches*

**What it is:** Track which activities give you energy (+) vs. drain you (-). Career sustainability depends on having enough net-positive energy in your work.

**When to use:** When someone is burned out, or when they keep succeeding at things that leave them empty.

**Process:**
- List your recent work activities over 2 weeks
- Mark each as energy-giving (+), neutral (0), or energy-draining (-)
- What patterns emerge? What roles/environments maximize the positives?

**Key insight:** Skills and energy are different. You can be skilled at something that drains you. The goal is work that plays to *both*.

---

## 9. 10,000 Hours + Deliberate Practice
*From Anders Ericsson (research); Malcolm Gladwell (popularization)*

**What it is:** Expert-level performance requires roughly 10,000 hours of *deliberate* practice — working at the edge of your current ability with focused feedback, not just logging time.

**When to use:** When someone wants to become genuinely excellent in a domain, or when calibrating how long a career transition will realistically take.

**Honest caveat:** Gladwell popularized this but oversimplified it. Ericsson's actual research shows the quality of practice matters far more than quantity. Also: some domains (creative, entrepreneurial) have different expertise curves than others (chess, sports).

---

## 10. BHAG + Backcasting
*BHAG from Jim Collins & Jerry Porras, "Built to Last"; Backcasting from John Robinson (sustainability planning)*

**What it is:**
- **BHAG** (Big Hairy Audacious Goal): A 10–25 year goal that's ambitious enough to be inspiring but specific enough to be directional.
- **Backcasting**: Start at the end state (the BHAG) and work backwards: what would need to be true in year 10? Year 5? Year 2? Year 1? Next 90 days?

**When to use:** When someone has long-term ambition but no roadmap, or when short-term steps feel disconnected from bigger goals.

**Contrast with forecasting:** Forecasting asks "given where I am, what's possible?" Backcasting asks "given where I want to be, what must be true?" The latter is more useful for ambitious goals.

## 11. The Portfolio Career
*From Charles Handy, "The Age of Unreason"*

**What it is:** Rather than a single linear career, think of your working life as a portfolio of different activities — paid work, project work, study work, gift work (volunteering), home work. The composition shifts over time.

**When to use:** When someone rejects the standard "climb the ladder" path, when they have multiple strong interests, or when they're thinking about career in mid-life.

**Honest limitation:** Portfolio careers require financial runway and often come with income volatility and benefit gaps. Don't romanticize them without grounding the conversation in practical realities.

---

## 12. Second-Order Thinking
*From Howard Marks, "The Most Important Thing"; also Charlie Munger*

**What it is:** First-order thinking asks "what happens if I do X?" Second-order thinking asks "...and then what? And then what after that?"

**When to use:** For any major career decision — especially ones that seem obviously good or obviously bad.

**Application:**
- "I'll take the higher-paying job at the big company" → and then? (Less learning, slower growth, golden handcuffs) → and then? (Hard to leave in 5 years, miss the window for early-stage companies)
- "I'll leave and start something" → and then? (Financial pressure in 12 months) → and then? (May need to take a worse job to survive)

---

## 13. Opportunity Cost Framing
*From economics; championed by Shane Parrish / Farnam Street*

**What it is:** Every choice forecloses other choices. The real cost of a decision isn't just its direct cost — it's the value of the best alternative you give up.

**When to use:** When someone is anchoring too heavily on what they're giving up (e.g., salary, status) without accounting for what they gain — or vice versa.

**Key question:** "What is the best alternative use of these next 3 years? How does this path compare?"

---

## 14. The Reverse Resume
*Adapted from Tim Ferriss and various coaches*

**What it is:** Instead of listing what you've done, list:
- What you *don't* want in your next role (anti-goals)
- What environments make you perform worst
- What types of work drain you most

**When to use:** When someone knows what they want to escape but can't articulate what they're moving toward. Constraints often reveal preferences better than open-ended questions.

---

## 15. Luck Surface Area
*From Jason Roberts*

**What it is:** Luck = Doing × Telling. Increase the number of interesting things you're working on AND the number of people who know what you're working on. Luck becomes more likely when you create more surface area for it to land on.

**When to use:** When someone feels their career is stagnant due to "lack of opportunities." Often the real issue is low surface area.

**Practical actions:** Writing publicly, attending conferences, reaching out to adjacent peers, contributing to communities, having a clear "I'm looking for X" message.

## Decision Matrix (Weighted Scoring)
*Common in management consulting; popularized for personal decisions by various coaches*

**What it is:** List your options as rows and your criteria as columns. Weight each criterion by importance (1–5). Score each option against each criterion (1–10). Multiply and sum to get a total per option.

**When to use:** Multi-option decisions with multiple competing criteria (e.g., choosing between job offers that differ on comp, learning, prestige, commute, mission).

**How to apply honestly:**
- Pick criteria *before* scoring options, not after — picking criteria to justify a preferred answer is the classic failure mode
- Force yourself to include criteria that argue against your gut preference
- The output is a thinking aid, not the decision. If the matrix says A but your gut screams B, that gap is worth examining, not overriding

**Honest limitation:** Numbers create false precision. Two options scoring 78 and 82 are not meaningfully different. Use the matrix to *surface* hidden weights and tradeoffs, not to crown a winner.

## Lean Startup Thinking (Applied to Careers)
*From Eric Ries, "The Lean Startup"; adapted to careers by various practitioners*

**What it is:** Treat a potential career path as a hypothesis to be tested with the smallest possible experiment, not as a decision to be made all at once. Build–Measure–Learn: define what you'd need to see to believe the path is right, design the cheapest experiment that would produce that signal, run it, update.

**When to use:** When someone is considering a big, risky, or expensive career move (starting a company, leaving a stable job, going back to school) and would benefit from a smaller test first.

**Application:**
- Considering a startup? Validate the problem with 20 customer conversations before quitting
- Considering a career switch to design? Take on three small projects before enrolling in a bootcamp
- Considering management? Lead a small project before applying for the title

**Key insight:** Most career anxiety comes from treating reversible decisions as irreversible. Cheap experiments collapse the uncertainty faster than more thinking.

**Honest limitation:** Some career moves can't be experimented with cheaply (medical school, military service, having children). Don't pretend everything is a hypothesis when some things are commitments.

## Framework Selection Guide

| User feeling/question | Try these frameworks |
|---|---|
| "I don't know what I want" | Ikigai, Energy Audit, Odyssey Plans |
| "I want to do too many things" | Hedgehog, Skills Stack, Opportunity Cost |
| "I'm scared to make a move" | Regret Minimization, BATNA, Second-Order Thinking |
| "I don't feel good enough" | Career Capital, Deliberate Practice, Skills Stack |
| "I want to grow long-term" | BHAG + Backcasting, Career Capital, Hedgehog |
| "I feel stuck/no opportunities" | Luck Surface Area, BATNA, Portfolio Career |
| "Should I take this offer?" | BATNA, Decision Matrix, Second-Order Thinking |
| "I want more meaning" | Ikigai, Energy Audit, Portfolio Career |
| "I want to start something" | Lean thinking, Personal Monopoly, Luck Surface Area |
