# Decision-Type Playbooks

Some career questions have their own structure. This file provides playbooks for the most common decision types — what to surface, what to watch out for, and which frameworks apply.

---

## Job Offer Evaluation

**When this applies:** The user has a specific offer in hand and is deciding whether to accept, negotiate, or decline.

**Key questions to surface:**
- What's the total comp, broken down? (Base, bonus target and structure, equity grant and vesting schedule, benefits, retirement match, ESPP if applicable)
- What's the role in 12 months — what would success look like, and who decides?
- Who is the direct manager? Have you had a real conversation with them?
- What's the company's financial situation? (For public: recent earnings trajectory. For private: funding stage, last round, runway — ask directly)
- What are you giving up by taking this? (The BATNA question)

**Equity literacy:** Most people can't evaluate equity because they don't know what questions to ask. RSUs at a public company are straightforward (shares that vest over time). Options at a private company are much harder: the strike price, the 409A valuation, the liquidation preference stack, and the dilution path between now and any exit all affect what the grant is actually worth. Double-trigger acceleration matters if the company gets acquired. The honest answer is usually that private company equity is worth somewhere between zero and its face value, and nobody knows which.

**The negotiation window:** Most offers have room to negotiate, and most candidates don't try. The window closes once you accept. Research market ranges before negotiating, name a specific number rather than asking what's possible, and get the negotiation done in one pass rather than going back repeatedly.

**Common failure modes:** Anchoring too heavily on base salary and underweighting learning, manager quality, and trajectory. Accepting under artificial time pressure without asking for more time to decide (you can almost always get 48–72 more hours). Treating equity projections at private companies as reliable income.

**Synthesis structure:** Decompose total comp, then explicitly rank what matters most to the user (comp vs. learning vs. role scope vs. team), then score the offer against those criteria honestly. Name the uncertainties.

Frameworks especially useful here: Decision Matrix, BATNA, Second-Order Thinking, Pre-mortem.

## Stay vs. Leave

**When this applies:** The user is unhappy enough to be considering leaving but hasn't made a decision yet.

**Key questions to surface:**
- What specifically is making you want to leave? (This matters — "the culture" and "my manager" have very different solutions)
- Is this problem specific to your team/manager, or is it structural to the company?
- Have you tried to change the situation directly? What happened?
- What would need to be true for this to be good? Is that achievable?
- What's your BATNA if you stay?

**Diagnosing the problem type:** The manager problem (fixable by transferring or waiting for a new manager), the role problem (fixable by moving internally), and the company problem (structural, not fixable without leaving) have meaningfully different responses. Many people treat a manager problem like a company problem and leave good situations unnecessarily. Some treat a company problem like a manager problem and stay too long.

**The cost of staying too long:** The research on this is unambiguous — people systematically underweight the cost of staying in a bad situation relative to the discomfort of leaving. Every year in a role that isn't building your career capital is an opportunity cost. This isn't about leaving impulsively; it's about being honest that staying has a cost too.

**Common failure modes:** Leaving before trying to fix the fixable thing. Staying because leaving feels uncomfortable, not because the situation is actually good. Making the decision reactively after a bad week rather than from a considered place.

**Synthesis structure:** First diagnose the problem type (manager/role/company). Then assess whether it's fixable. Then evaluate the BATNA honestly. Present the options as: fix it, wait it out, leave now, leave in X months after improving your BATNA.

Frameworks especially useful here: BATNA, Regret Minimization, Second-Order Thinking, Opportunity Cost.

## IC vs. Management Track

**When this applies:** The user is considering moving into people management, or is already a manager and questioning whether it's right for them.

**Key questions to surface:**
- What specifically appeals to you about management? (Check for borrowed desire — management is often wanted because it signals status, not because the work fits)
- Have you had any experience leading or coordinating a team, even informally?
- What do you actually enjoy most in your current work? How much of that survives in management?
- In your company's structure, what does "senior IC" look like? Is that path real and respected?

**What actually changes in management:** Your output is no longer your own work — it's the work of your team. Deep work shrinks. Your calendar fills with 1:1s, planning sessions, and cross-functional coordination. The skills that made you excellent as an IC (speed, technical depth, individual execution) are less relevant. The skills that make you excellent as a manager (building trust, developing people, running good processes, absorbing ambiguity) are different ones that you may or may not have.

**Testing before committing:** Lead a project. Manage an intern. Volunteer to mentor. These small experiments reveal a lot. If you don't enjoy the coordination and people work in these smaller doses, a full management role won't be better.

**The player-coach trap:** Many new managers try to keep doing significant IC work alongside managing. It usually means doing both things worse. The transition is harder to complete in this mode.

**Switching back:** It's possible in most organizations and industries, but it does carry a perception cost in some. Research the norms in your specific field.

Frameworks especially useful here: Lean Startup Thinking (test before committing), Energy Audit, Anti-Mimetic Thinking, Career Capital.

## Founding vs. Joining a Startup

**When this applies:** The user is deciding whether to start something themselves or join an early-stage company.

**Key questions to surface (founding):**
- Do you have a specific problem you're obsessed with, or is the appeal more about the identity of "being a founder"?
- Do you have a co-founder, or are you planning to go it alone? (Solo founding is significantly harder — research this)
- What's your financial runway? How long can you go without income?
- Have you talked to 20+ potential customers about the problem?

**Key questions to surface (joining):**
- What stage is the company? (Seed, Series A, B, C — the risk/return profile changes dramatically)
- How many months of runway do they have? (Ask directly. If they won't tell you, that's a signal)
- What's the equity grant as a percentage of fully diluted shares, not just number of options? What's the vesting schedule?
- What's the leadership team's track record? Have they done this before?

**Founding:** The upside is real but the base rate of success is low, and survivorship bias severely distorts how the path looks from outside. The conditions that make founding more likely to succeed: deep domain knowledge, a co-founder you trust, a clear customer problem (not a solution looking for a problem), and enough financial runway to reach real signal. Missing two or more of these significantly changes the odds.

**Joining early-stage:** The comp is typically below market; the equity compensates only if there's a real exit. The learning velocity at a well-run early-stage company can be exceptional — you see everything and own more than you would elsewhere. Evaluate the team and the problem as carefully as the equity.

**Common failure modes (founding):** Starting because you're unhappy at your job, not because you're obsessed with the problem. Under-estimating how long the pre-revenue phase takes. **Common failure modes (joining):** Accepting an offer based on the equity story without understanding the cap table or dilution math.

Frameworks especially useful here: Lean Startup Thinking, Pre-mortem, BATNA, Second-Order Thinking.

## Going Independent (Freelance / Consulting / Solopreneur)

**When this applies:** The user is considering leaving employment to work for themselves, whether as a freelancer, independent consultant, or solo business owner.

**Key questions to surface:**
- Do you have an existing network that would send you work? (The pipeline question — most people underestimate how central this is)
- What's your runway? How many months can you cover expenses without income?
- Have you done any freelance work on the side? What happened?
- What's your minimum viable revenue to survive, and what's your target revenue?

**Pipeline math:** Revenue = leads × close rate × deal size. Most people starting out focus on the work and not the pipeline. The steady state requires continuous investment in relationships and visibility, even when you're busy. The feast-or-famine cycle is common and manageable once you understand it, but it surprises almost everyone the first time.

**Runway requirements:** Six months is the minimum most advisors suggest before going fully independent. Twelve is more comfortable. This includes accounting for the gap between when you do the work and when you get paid (net-30 to net-60 payment terms are common and can create serious cash flow problems).

**The lonely middle:** After the initial excitement and before the business stabilizes, there's typically a difficult period where the novelty has worn off and the revenue isn't consistent yet. This phase lasts different amounts of time for different people. Knowing it's coming helps.

**Tax and admin overhead:** Self-employment tax, quarterly estimated taxes, health insurance, retirement contributions, and accounting software all add up to meaningful cost and time. Budget for them before going in.

Frameworks especially useful here: BATNA, Lean Startup Thinking (test with side work first), Opportunity Cost, Second-Order Thinking.

## Geographic Moves

**When this applies:** The user is considering relocating for a career reason, or a geographic constraint is affecting their career options.

**Key questions to surface:**
- Is this move driven by career, personal life, or both? (The calculus is different)
- What does the job market for your field look like in the target location? (Research this honestly — don't assume it's similar to where you are)
- What happens to your current professional network if you move?
- Have you run the actual cost-of-living numbers, including housing, taxes, and childcare if relevant?

**Networks are local:** This is consistently underestimated. The professional relationships that generate opportunities — introductions, referrals, informal intel — are built in person over time. Moving to a new city means starting that over. Remote work has softened this for some roles and not at all for others. Research whether the specific field requires in-person presence to build the relationships that matter.

**Remote doesn't fully solve it:** For senior roles in many fields, "remote" still means occasional travel for key meetings, offsite strategy sessions, and relationship-building moments. It also means you're competing with everyone in the company's geography for advancement, while being present for fewer of the informal moments where decisions get made.

**Cost-of-living math:** Rent is the number people look at; tax rates, childcare costs, commute costs, and housing transaction costs are the numbers that bite. Run a complete budget, not just the rent comparison.

**Long-term tradeoff:** Some locations offer career acceleration (more density of opportunity, faster learning, better networks) at the cost of personal quality of life. Some offer quality of life at the cost of career pace. Neither is wrong — but the tradeoff should be made consciously, not discovered after the move.

Frameworks especially useful here: Odyssey Plans, Opportunity Cost, Decision Matrix, Second-Order Thinking.
