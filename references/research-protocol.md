# Research Protocol for Career Questions

This guide tells you *what* to search for and *how* to synthesize findings when researching career options. Always follow this before giving substantive advice on a specific role, industry, or transition path.

---

## Core Principle: State Your Uncertainty

> **A note on dates in search queries:** Examples below use `[current year]` as a placeholder. Substitute the actual current year when running searches, and consider also searching the previous year (`[current year - 1]`) to catch coverage that hasn't been updated yet.

Before searching, acknowledge what you don't know:
> "Before I give you a view on [X], let me research current market conditions — my training data may be outdated."

After searching, be explicit about what you found vs. didn't find:
> "I found clear data on [Y], but [Z] was harder to verify — treat that part with more skepticism."

## Research Templates by Question Type

### 1. Role Exploration ("What does a [role] actually do?")

**Search for:**
- `[role title] day in the life [current year] OR [current year - 1]`
- `[role title] responsibilities vs. expectations`
- `[role title] career path progression`
- `[role title] skills required vs. skills listed job postings`

**Synthesize:**
- What do people *actually* do day-to-day vs. what the job description says?
- What's the hardest part of the role that isn't obvious from the outside?
- What career paths do people take after this role?

**Honest caveats to surface:**
- Job titles vary enormously across companies (a "Product Manager" at a startup ≠ PM at Google)
- Day-in-the-life accounts online skew toward people who want to attract others to the field
- Actual experience depends heavily on company, team, and manager

---

### 2. Industry Conditions ("Is [industry] a good bet right now?")

**Search for:**
- `[industry] job market [current year]`
- `[industry] hiring trends [current year]`
- `[industry] layoffs OR growth [current year]`
- `[industry] AI impact on jobs [current year]` (if relevant)
- `[industry] outlook next 5 years`

**Synthesize:**
- Is the industry growing, contracting, or restructuring?
- What parts of the industry are growing vs. shrinking?
- What's the effect of AI/automation on entry and mid-level roles?
- Are there geographic concentrations that matter?

**Honest caveats to surface:**
- "Industry outlook" articles are often written by people with a stake in the industry's growth
- Published reports lag reality by 6–18 months
- Individual companies within an industry vary enormously
- The user's local market may differ from national/global trends

---

### 3. Salary & Compensation ("What does this pay?")

**Search for:**
- `[role title] salary [location OR "United States"] [current year]`
- `[role title] compensation levels [company type: startup vs. FAANG vs. mid-market]`
- `[role title] total compensation vs. base salary`
- Site-specific: levels.fyi (tech), glassdoor, linkedin salary, payscale

> **International sources:** Levels.fyi (tech, increasingly international), Otta / Welcome to the Jungle (Europe), kununu (DACH region), JobBank (Canada), PayScale (international), Salary Expert. Glassdoor data quality varies sharply outside the US. When researching for a user outside the US, prioritize country-specific sources and acknowledge data thinness.

**Synthesize:**
- Median base salary range (always a range, never a single number)
- How much variance exists by location, company size, and level?
- Is total compensation (equity, bonus) meaningfully different from base?
- How does this compare to the user's current compensation?

**Honest caveats to surface:**
- Self-reported salary data has selection bias (higher earners more likely to share)
- Equity value is highly uncertain and should not be counted as reliable income
- Geographic variance is enormous — a "good salary" in SF is very different from Dallas
- Salary data ages quickly — always note the source date

---

### 4. Transition Paths ("Can I get from [A] to [B]?")

**Search for:**
- `how to transition from [current role] to [target role]`
- `[target role] career change from [background]`
- `[target role] entry requirements no experience`
- LinkedIn: search for people who have made this transition (note patterns)

**Synthesize:**
- Is this transition commonly done? (Or is it rare/exceptional?)
- What's the typical pathway? (Direct, via a bridge role, via education/certification)
- What's the realistic timeline?
- What are the common failure modes or dead-ends?

**Honest caveats to surface:**
- Success stories are vastly over-represented online vs. failed attempts
- "How I did it" advice often generalizes from unusual personal circumstances
- Some transitions require credentials that have real time/cost constraints
- Some transitions are nearly impossible without specific networks or geography

---

### 5. Skill Gap Analysis ("What skills do I need to get there?")

**Search for:**
- `[target role] required skills [current year]`
- `[target role] job postings [location]` — scan 5–10 actual postings
- `[target role] what hiring managers look for`
- `[target role] portfolio OR credentials required`

**Synthesize:**
- What skills appear in >80% of job postings? (These are table stakes)
- What skills are rare but highly valued? (These create differentiation)
- What credentials are actually required vs. "nice to have"?
- What can be self-taught vs. requires formal education?

**Honest caveats to surface:**
- Job postings often list aspirational requirements ("5 years experience" for a new field)
- Credentials listed ≠ credentials actually evaluated in hiring
- "Soft skills" in postings are real — don't skip them because they're vague
- Skills requirements change fast in technical fields; verify with recent postings

---

### 6. Company/Opportunity Research ("Should I join [company]?")

**Search for:**
- `[company name] culture review [current year]`
- `[company name] layoffs OR growth [current year]`
- `[company name] Glassdoor reviews [role type]`
- `[company name] financial health` (for private: funding stage, burn rate; for public: recent earnings)
- `[company name] leadership news [current year]`

**Synthesize:**
- Is the company financially stable? Growing or shrinking?
- What are employees saying about culture and management?
- Is there recent leadership or strategic instability?
- What's the typical tenure at this company? (High churn is a signal)

**Honest caveats to surface:**
- Glassdoor reviews are biased toward strong positive and strong negative experiences
- "Culture" varies enormously by team and manager within the same company
- Funding stage and burn rate are often private information — you'll have imperfect data
- Recent layoffs can mean either distress or restructuring for growth — context matters

## How to Present Research Findings

Always structure research findings like this:

### What I searched for
> "I searched for [queries] to understand [question]."

### What I found (with confidence levels)
> "**High confidence:** [X] — this was consistent across multiple sources.
> **Medium confidence:** [Y] — I found some data on this but sources varied.
> **Low confidence / unclear:** [Z] — I couldn't find reliable recent data on this."

### What this means for your situation
> "Given [user's context], this suggests [implication]. The key uncertainty is [X]."

### What I couldn't verify
> "I wasn't able to find reliable data on [X]. You might get better signal by [talking to someone in the field / checking LinkedIn profiles / informational interviews]."

### When sources contradict

Don't average contradictory data into mush. Instead:
- Name the contradiction explicitly: "Glassdoor reports X; LinkedIn data suggests Y."
- Look for the meta-pattern: is one source biased (selection effects, who reports)? Is one more recent? Is the contradiction real (e.g., the role varies hugely by company)?
- When you can't resolve it, surface that to the user: "These don't agree, and I can't tell you which is closer to truth. Here's what I'd do to find out."

## When Research Is Insufficient

Some questions can't be answered well by web search. Flag these explicitly:

- **"Is this the right culture fit for me?"** → No search will answer this. Recommend informational interviews or job shadowing.
- **"Will I be good at this?"** → Research can inform, but only real experience (projects, freelance, volunteer work) can validate.
- **"What's it actually like to work at [company]?"** → Glassdoor helps but talking to current/former employees is vastly more valuable.
- **"Will this industry exist in 10 years?"** → Nobody knows. Present the bull and bear cases honestly.

For these, your job is to **help the user figure out how to get better information**, not to pretend you have it.

---

## Helping the User Run Informational Interviews

When research can't answer the question (culture, fit, what it's really like), the next best signal is a 30-minute conversation with someone who's done the thing. Help the user structure these:

**Finding people:**
- LinkedIn search for the target role + "open to chat" or alumni from the user's school/company
- Industry-specific Slack communities, Discord servers, subreddits
- Twitter/X for some industries; Substack comment sections for others
- Warm intros from the user's existing network beat cold outreach 5:1

**Outreach pattern that gets replies:**
- Short (under 100 words)
- Specific about why this person (referenced their work, role, or path)
- Asks for 20–30 minutes, not "advice" or "to pick your brain"
- Includes one concrete question they could answer if they don't want to meet

**Questions worth asking (in this order):**
1. How did you actually get into this role? (Not the LinkedIn version)
2. What's the hardest part that isn't obvious from the outside?
3. Who succeeds in this role and who doesn't? What's the difference?
4. If you were me, what would you do next?
5. Who else should I talk to?

**Synthesizing across conversations:**
- One conversation is an anecdote; three is a pattern; five is signal
- Track contradictions explicitly — they reveal what varies by company/team vs. what's structural to the role

---

## Red Flags in Research

Be suspicious when:
- All the positive information comes from the company itself
- Salary data is very old (>2 years) or comes from a single source
- "Success stories" for a transition path seem unusually exceptional
- Industry outlook is uniformly optimistic (who wrote this? who benefits?)
- A role or path is described as "easy" to break into

Always ask: **Who wrote this, and what are their incentives?**
