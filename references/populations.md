# Population-Specific Guidance

The rest of this skill implicitly assumes a mid-career knowledge worker with reasonable optionality. That's not always who's in the conversation. This file notes where the standard approach needs adjusting for specific populations.

---

## Early Career (0–3 Years of Experience)

Typical questions: "Should I leave my first job?" "Is this company good for my career?" "How do I know if I'm on the right track?"

At this stage, Career Capital matters more than Regret Minimization. The cost of a wrong move is low — a year at a bad job still teaches you something about what you don't want. The framework to resist is the one that says "you must find your passion and follow it now." Most people don't know what they're good at yet because they haven't tried enough.

What matters most: is this role giving you real work at the edge of your ability, with feedback from people better than you? If the answer is no for more than 6–12 months, that's a better reason to leave than "I don't feel fulfilled."

The 70/20/10 split is especially corrective here. Early-career workers over-index on courses and certifications hoping they'll create options. The options come from the work and the relationships more reliably.

On the "when to leave" question: leaving a first job after less than a year is a yellow flag to future employers in most fields — not disqualifying, but worth having a clear story for. Staying 18–24 months before moving is a reasonable floor in most industries.

## Career Returners (After Leave for Parenting, Illness, Caregiving, Sabbatical)

Typical questions: "How do I explain the gap?" "Am I too far behind?" "Will anyone take me seriously after X years out?"

The gap itself is rarely the barrier returners think it is. The actual barriers are usually two: confidence atrophy (from time outside a professional identity) and network atrophy (the contacts who would have referred them have moved on). Both are real and both are fixable, but they take longer than people want.

On framing the gap: matter-of-fact beats apologetic every time. "I took three years off to care for a parent" is a complete sentence. The urge to over-explain or pre-justify invites more scrutiny, not less.

Structured returnship programs reduce the re-entry friction significantly. Programs worth researching: Path Forward (tech and financial services), ReBoot Accel (tech-focused), iRelaunch (broad industries), and company-specific returnship programs at large employers (search "[company] returnship program"). These programs are designed for exactly this situation and typically convert to full-time roles at meaningful rates — research current numbers for the user's target sector.

The frameworks that apply most here are Career Capital (reactivating and updating skills) and Luck Surface Area (rebuilding network visibility). Regret Minimization is less useful — the leave already happened; the question now is what to do from here.

## Forced Transitions (Layoffs, Performance Plans, Restructuring)

Typical questions: "What do I do now?" "Is my severance fair?" "Should I take the first offer I get?"

There's an emotional sequence that's nearly universal: shock, then an identity hit (especially if the person's identity was strongly tied to the company or role), then a gradual reorientation toward what's next. Trying to skip the middle part by jumping straight to job applications usually produces worse decisions and a flatter search. Acknowledge where the person is in this sequence before going tactical.

On severance: standard is typically one to two weeks per year of service, but this varies enormously by company, level, and whether you negotiate. Severance agreements often require signing away legal claims — worth having an employment attorney review before signing anything that feels off, especially at senior levels. Research current norms for the user's industry and company size rather than guessing.

The "told my story" tax is real: every interview requires re-explaining the layoff. "The company had a reduction in force affecting my team" is accurate and neutral. Rehearse it until it's short and flat, not defensive.

On timing: runway math matters. How many months of expenses are covered by severance plus savings? That number determines how much time you have to be selective. If the answer is two months, the calculus is different than if it's eight. BATNA is the central framework here — the question isn't "should I hold out for something great," it's "what is my actual alternative if I don't take this offer?"

## Constrained Workers

Typical questions: "Can I even consider switching jobs with my visa situation?" "How do I find remote roles that work with my schedule?" "What do I do when my options are genuinely limited?"

This section covers workers navigating real structural constraints: immigration status, caregiving obligations, disability or chronic illness, or other conditions that aren't just "mindset" problems and can't be coached away.

**Immigration:** H-1B portability allows switching employers after I-485 is pending for 180+ days, but the specifics depend on the user's exact status and timeline — this requires an immigration attorney, not a career coach. The important framing: some roles and companies are substantially better at sponsoring green cards than others. This is worth researching explicitly as a filter on opportunities, not an afterthought. PERM timelines vary enormously by country of birth; build the plan around real timelines, not optimistic ones.

**Caregiving obligations:** Geographic and schedule constraints are real constraints, not failures of imagination. The framework is Opportunity Cost — given that certain options are off the table, what's the best available path within the actual constraints? Remote work has expanded the option set meaningfully for some roles and not at all for others. Research the specific field honestly.

**Disability and chronic illness:** Energy budgeting is a real career planning consideration. The Energy Audit framework applies literally here, not just metaphorically. On disclosure: there's no universal answer — it depends on the condition, the role, the employer culture, and what accommodations are needed. Researching the ADA (US) or equivalent protections in the user's jurisdiction is more useful than general advice about "being authentic."

The overarching principle: work within the real constraints, not an idealized version of the situation. Advice that ignores a constraint isn't advice — it's a fantasy.

## Mid-Career Switchers (Changing Industries or Functions)

Typical questions: "Is it too late to change fields?" "How do I get credit for what I've done?" "How long will this actually take?"

The credibility gap is the central challenge: you have skills, but no visible track record in the target field. Employers hire on pattern recognition, and your pattern doesn't match what they're looking for. The bridge role strategy addresses this — find a role that exists at the intersection of your current skills and the target field, even if it's not the destination. It's slower, but it works more reliably than trying to jump directly.

Portfolio building matters in fields where work is demonstrable (design, engineering, writing, data science). In fields where it isn't (strategy, operations, general management), the bridge is usually relationships — getting referred in by someone who knows your work.

On timeline: end-to-end transitions from "deciding to switch" to "comfortably established in the new field" typically take 12–24 months, sometimes longer. This is not pessimism — it's what the process actually takes when you account for skill-building, job search, and the learning curve in the new role. People who plan for 3 months and experience 18 months often interpret their own competence as the problem. It usually isn't.

The Skills Stack framework is useful here: identify which of your existing skills transfer directly, which transfer partially, and which you'll need to build from scratch. The first two categories are larger than most switchers think.

## Late Career and Encore Careers

Typical questions: "What do I do after a long career?" "How do I stay relevant?" "Is there still something worth doing?"

This population is underserved by most career advice, which is written for people climbing. The questions here are different: how to use accumulated expertise without a traditional employer relationship, how to shift from execution to influence, and how to make meaning out of what's next.

Fractional and advisory roles are increasingly viable for senior practitioners. The model is portfolio-like — several part-time engagements rather than one full-time role. The transition requires a real shift: selling yourself as an advisor rather than an employee, building pipeline through relationships rather than job applications, and accepting income variability. The Portfolio Career framework applies directly.

Board seats and advisory roles follow networks, not job boards. The path is relationship-driven. Research which boards in the user's sector are actively seeking members with their background — governance needs vary.

Knowledge transfer and mentorship work are often more satisfying to this population than they expect. The question worth asking: what do you know that almost no one else does, and who would benefit from having access to it? That question often points toward what's next more reliably than any framework.

Age discrimination is real in some sectors and negligible in others. Research this honestly for the user's target field rather than reassuring them it won't be a factor.
